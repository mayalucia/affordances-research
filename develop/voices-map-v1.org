* Note to mayadev (delete on publish)

This is the card source for =playgrounds/voices.html=. Each voice
has five fields:

- *the move* — one sentence: what this voice contributes that no
  other voice does.
- *the tension* — one sentence: who they disagree with / answer /
  are answered by inside the same map.
- *the source* — one bravli file path + line range the curious
  reader can jump into, deeper than the card.
- *the landmark* — the single book or paper the source section is
  built on (citation, not gloss).
- *what SCENE inherits* — one sentence: picks up the question the
  essay closes each section with.

The landmark field was promoted out of /the source/ on the v1.1
substrate pass — landmarks are citation-grade and warrant their
own slot. Single-edit point if a citation needs to shift (e.g.
Friston 2006 → 2010 NRN review).

Scope decision: *all fourteen voices in v1*. Rationale in the
reply message. Clusters are five, not the seven you guessed — see
the cluster intros. The cluster /intros/ are optional scaffold
text; tangle them or drop them, your call.

Voice order within a cluster is the order the essay introduces
them, which is also the order of intellectual debt (Gibson →
Helmholtz is the founding pair; Marr → Jaynes → Friston is the
normative ladder).

The bravli substrate file is =codev/voices-on-affordance.org=
(3541 lines). I cite line ranges in /the source/ so the curious
reader lands inside the right section.

* Map-level frame

** The opening claim

Fourteen voices are not fourteen theories of the same phenomenon.
They are fourteen questions about the same phenomenon — and each
question gets a different answer when asked of the same mouse, in
the same arena, doing the same task.

** What the map is for

To stand on the floor of the workshop. To see who is leaning into
whom. To find which voice answers your question (and which voice
would answer it differently).

* Cluster 1 — Perception
:PROPERTIES:
:CLUSTER:  perception
:QUESTION: What is perceived?
:END:

The founding pair. Gibson says the world is already structured for
action; the system resonates. Helmholtz says the world is
underdetermined by the senses; the system infers. SCENE inherits
Gibson's word /affordance/ and Helmholtz's tools /(latent
representations, inverse problems, internal models)/. The tension
between them is the intellectual engine of the entire programme.

** Gibson
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   gibson
:CLUSTER: perception
:END:

*** the move
The structure that makes action possible is /already/ in the optic
array — no internal representation is needed to recover what the
animal needs to do.

*** the tension
Helmholtz answers him directly: the optic array is ambiguous;
inference is unavoidable. Friston later /unifies/ both readings
(the resonance Gibson sees is the inference Helmholtz demands,
running in active-inference form), but the unification is itself
contested.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 31–100 (Gibson section).

*** the landmark
/Ecological Approach to Visual Perception/ (1979).

*** what SCENE inherits
The concept itself (affordance), the ecological validity demand
(test with natural stimuli, in arenas, with bodies), and the
insistence that perception is /for/ action.

** Helmholtz
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   helmholtz
:CLUSTER: perception
:END:

*** the move
Perception is /unconscious inference/ — the brain reconstructs
distal causes from proximal effects using internal models.

*** the tension
Gibson denies the inference is necessary; Bialek shows the
inference is information-theoretically near-optimal; Friston turns
the inference into the unifying principle. Almost every later
voice is some flavour of Helmholtz with extra constraints.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 101–205 (Helmholtz
section).

*** the landmark
/Treatise on Physiological Optics/ (1867).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /computational framework/ — internal models, belief states,
posterior over hidden causes — and with it the legitimacy of asking
CEBRA-style questions about latent structure in neural recordings.

* Cluster 2 — Optimality
:PROPERTIES:
:CLUSTER:  optimality
:QUESTION: How well, and at what cost?
:END:

The /quantitative/ voices. Bialek measures how close to the
physical limit the nervous system gets at extracting information;
Shannon supplies the currency (mutual information) and the
unbreakable bounds (channel capacity, rate-distortion); Sterling
and Laughlin add the constraint that makes the question empirical
— the nervous system pays for every bit in glucose and volume, so
it cannot afford to compute everything, only the affordance-relevant
things.

** Bialek
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   bialek
:CLUSTER: optimality
:END:

*** the move
The nervous system extracts information from sensory signals at or
near the physical limits set by the noise — and we know this
because we have measured it (fly photoreceptor, cricket cercal
system, retinal ganglion cell).

*** the tension
Braitenberg suspects this is overkill — maybe a much simpler
circuit would suffice for the actual behaviour. Sterling and
Laughlin /agree/ that the system is optimal but insist the
optimality is constrained by metabolic cost, not just by the
physics of noise.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 206–345 (Bialek section).

*** the landmark
/Biophysics: Searching for Principles/ (2012).

*** what SCENE inherits
The quantitative benchmark: latent structure should carry mutual
information with task variables /near/ the information-theoretic
limit set by the input statistics — and this is testable.

** Shannon
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   shannon
:CLUSTER: optimality
:END:

*** the move
Communication has hard limits — channel capacity, rate-distortion,
the data processing inequality — and the nervous system, whatever
it is doing, is doing it inside those limits.

*** the tension
Shannon is the only voice who refuses to take sides between Gibson
and Helmholtz: he asks /how many bits/, not /what the bits mean/.
Braitenberg complements him from below (the simplest channel that
suffices); Sterling–Laughlin extend him with /cost per bit/.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 469–682 (Shannon section).

*** the landmark
/A Mathematical Theory of Communication/ (1948).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /currency/ (mutual information), the /bounds/ (capacity,
rate-distortion), and the discipline of the data processing
inequality — compression can only lose, never create.

** Sterling–Laughlin
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   sterling-laughlin
:CLUSTER: optimality
:END:

*** the move
The nervous system is an /engineering design/ under joint energy
and volume constraints — its architecture reflects what it can
afford to invest in, not what it could in principle compute.

*** the tension
Friston unifies them in free-energy minimisation (cost is part of
the principle); Bialek says optimality alone is the constraint;
Marder warns that the "design" looks more like a population of
homeostatically-stabilised solutions than a clean engineered point.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 683–870 (Sterling–Laughlin
section).

*** the landmark
/Principles of Neural Design/ (2015).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /budget constraint/ — the prediction that the nervous system
invests in affordance-relevant features and economises on
everything else, and the empirical test of which dimensions of
latent structure track what the system has actually paid for.

* Cluster 3 — Inference
:PROPERTIES:
:CLUSTER:  inference
:QUESTION: What problem is being solved?
:END:

The /normative/ voices. Marr insists you specify the computational
theory before asking how. Jaynes specifies what /counts/ as
inference (probability theory, the maximum-entropy prior, the
posterior-plus-loss-equals-action discipline). Friston offers the
single principle that unifies perception, action, and learning
under a single variational objective — and pays for the unification
in falsifiability.

** Marr
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   marr
:CLUSTER: inference
:END:

*** the move
Three levels of analysis — computational, algorithmic,
implementational — and you /must/ specify the first before the
others make sense.

*** the tension
Gibson sits at the computational level; Helmholtz at the
algorithmic; Bialek at the implementational. Marr is therefore the
voice who says /the other voices are all answering different
questions/ — and refuses to let you confuse them.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 1351–1584 (Marr section).

*** the landmark
/Vision/ (1982).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /methodological discipline/: write down the computational
theory of each affordance — what function is being computed? —
before decoding neural data. Without it, CEBRA analysis is
ungrounded correlation-hunting.

** Jaynes
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   jaynes
:CLUSTER: inference
:END:

*** the move
Probability /is/ the logic of inference (not a description of
randomness in the world) — and the maximum-entropy prior, combined
with a loss function, determines the optimal action uniquely.

*** the tension
Sterling–Laughlin says the organism cannot afford the optimal
posterior; Friston converts Jaynes's inference into a /variational/
inference; Marr puts Jaynes inside Level 1 as the normative
computational theory.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 1118–1350 (Jaynes section).

*** the landmark
/Probability Theory: The Logic of Science/ (2003).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /decision-theoretic frame/: posterior + loss = optimal action,
the demand that you specify the model /before/ inference, and
(unexpectedly) the body-relative scaling of affordances as a
consequence of transformation-group symmetry, not biological
contingency.

** Friston
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   friston
:CLUSTER: inference
:END:

*** the move
A single variational principle — minimisation of free energy —
unifies perception, action, learning, and policy selection in one
mathematics: existence /is/ surprisal minimisation.

*** the tension
Gibson is /resolved into/ active inference (the resonance is
prediction-error minimisation by acting); Bialek is /constrained by/
free energy (the bound is information-theoretic); but Marder, Ashby,
and Bernstein each say the unification is too tidy — actual
biology has degeneracy, regulation, biomechanics that resist the
single objective.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 871–1117 (Friston section).

*** the landmark
/A Free Energy Principle for the Brain/ (2006).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /action-perception symmetry/ (both are prediction-error
minimisation), the language of policies and expected free energy,
and the open question of whether the principle is empirically
falsifiable in CEBRA-style experiments.

* Cluster 4 — Implementation depth
:PROPERTIES:
:CLUSTER:  implementation
:QUESTION: How simply could this be computed?
:END:

Two voices, one ladder. Braitenberg supplies the /null hypothesis/:
show me the simplest wiring that produces the behaviour, before
invoking representation. Bach supplies the /ceiling/: a stack of
five levels (reactive → associative → predictive → constructive →
reflective) that organises /what kind/ of computation a behaviour
might require. Together they bracket the implementation-depth
question.

** Braitenberg
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   braitenberg
:CLUSTER: implementation
:END:

*** the move
Many apparently-complex behaviours emerge from /very/ simple wiring
— the right test of any "the brain represents X" claim is whether
a Braitenberg vehicle with no representation of X also produces
the behaviour.

*** the tension
Helmholtz, Friston, and Bach require representation; Braitenberg
demands you show why a wire-and-threshold model fails before
invoking a representation. Marder agrees with him in /spirit/ (do
not over-interpret circuits) and with Helmholtz in /substance/ (the
circuits are richer than they look).

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 346–468 (Braitenberg
section).

*** the landmark
/Vehicles: Experiments in Synthetic Psychology/ (1984).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /null hypothesis discipline/: for every CEBRA latent dimension
the team claims represents an affordance, propose the Braitenberg
vehicle that would produce the same behaviour without that latent.

** Bach
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   bach
:CLUSTER: implementation
:END:

*** the move
Intelligence is /modelling/ — and the modelling can be stratified
into a stack of computational depths (reactive, associative,
predictive, constructive, reflective) that determines what the
organism can do.

*** the tension
Braitenberg sits at the bottom of Bach's stack (reactive); Friston
threads through it (predictive); Levin extends it sideways (the
stack runs at non-neural scales). Marr would insist Bach has
muddled levels of analysis with levels of mechanism.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 1811–2046 (Bach section).

*** the landmark
/Principles of Synthetic Intelligence/ (2009).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /modelling-stack taxonomy/ — which level (reactive, predictive,
constructive) is the mouse operating at in each affordance task?
— and the challenge of distinguishing interpolation within a known
model from construction of a new one.

* Cluster 5 — Body, regulator, substrate
:PROPERTIES:
:CLUSTER:  body
:QUESTION: Where is the affordance, really?
:END:

The four voices who refuse to let the brain hog the explanation.
Bernstein opens the body — biomechanics, synergies, degrees of
freedom — and finds the affordance boundary partly inside it. Ashby
says the whole apparatus exists to /regulate/ essential variables,
and supplies the cybernetic constraints (requisite variety, good
regulator theorem, ultrastability). Levin extends "agent" beyond
organisms with nervous systems (cells, tissues, bioelectric
networks). Marder warns that whatever elegant theory you build,
the circuit implementing it will be different in every animal,
will shift with internal state, and will drift over days.

** Bernstein
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   bernstein
:CLUSTER: body
:END:

*** the move
The body is /not/ a marionette — it is a dynamical system with its
own physics, redundant degrees of freedom, and synergies the
nervous system must exploit, not override.

*** the tension
Every other voice treats the body as a black box that "acts".
Bernstein opens the box. Bach and Friston build internal models;
Bernstein insists the most consequential internal model is of the
/body itself/. Ashby agrees: the body /is/ part of the regulator.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 2047–2322 (Bernstein
section).

*** the landmark
/On Dexterity and Its Development/ (1996).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /body half/ of the affordance equation — the prediction that
the affordance boundary tracks the synergy manifold (not just
visual features), and the demand that biomechanical state
(fatigue, injury, load) appear in any model of affordance change.

** Ashby
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   ashby
:CLUSTER: body
:END:

*** the move
Living systems are /regulators/ of essential variables, and the
law of requisite variety puts a hard mathematical limit on what
any regulator can do.

*** the tension
Friston subsumes Ashby (free-energy minimisation /is/ regulation);
Marr would put Ashby at Level 1 (the computational theory /is/
regulation); Marder warns that the same regulation can be
implemented by many degenerate circuits, so the apparent
ultrastability /is/ already homeostatic.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 2323–2633 (Ashby section).

*** the landmark
/An Introduction to Cybernetics/ (1956).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /regulatory frame/ — essential variables, viability bounds,
requisite variety as a dimensionality test, and the good regulator
theorem (which says the system /must/ contain a model of what it
regulates, whether anyone designed it to or not).

** Levin
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   levin
:CLUSTER: body
:END:

*** the move
Intelligence is /substrate-independent/ — goal-directed navigation
of state space happens at cellular and tissue scales, not just
neural — and the principles must be general enough to cover all of
them.

*** the tension
Helmholtz, Friston, Marr, Bach all build their stories around
neurons; Levin says the principles must be neuron-agnostic. Bernstein
quietly agrees: the body's biomechanics are non-neural intelligence
already. Marder agrees from below: even within a nervous system,
the implementation is wildly degenerate.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 1585–1810 (Levin section).

*** the landmark
/Technological Approach to Mind Everywhere/ (2022).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /substrate-generality challenge/ — if the principles SCENE
extracts from mice cannot, in principle, also describe a
xenobot's affordances, the principles are too narrow; if they
can, what does that say about what CEBRA latents actually mean?

** Marder
:PROPERTIES:
:VOICE:   marder
:CLUSTER: body
:END:

*** the move
The same function is implemented by many circuits (/degeneracy/)
and the same circuit implements many functions
(/multifunctionality/) — so do /not/ average across animals or
across days.

*** the tension
Bialek and Sterling–Laughlin assume the nervous system has /a/
solution; Marder shows it has a population of solutions stabilised
by homeostatic regulation. Marr would say the variability is in
the algorithm/implementation, not the computation — Marder says
sometimes the variability /is/ the computation.

*** the source
=codev/voices-on-affordance.org=, lines 2634–2910 (Marder
section).

*** the landmark
Prinz, Bucher & Marder (2004); Marder & Goaillard (2006).

*** what SCENE inherits
The /individual-animal discipline/: do not collapse across mice;
expect circuit drift across days; expect neuromodulatory
state-dependence within a session — and the prediction that the
affordance code itself is a moving target.
